'Earthquake' is a term used to describe both sudden slip on a fault, and the resulting ground shaking and radiated seismic energy caused by the slip, or by volcanic or magmatic activity, or other sudden stress changes in the earth. (US Geological Survey)
A fault is a fracture along which the blocks of crust on either side have moved relative to one another parallel to the fracture. (US Geological Survey)

Richter Scale

"Micro"
Micro earthquakes, not felt.
2.0 - 2.9
"Minor"
Generally not felt, but recorded.
3.0 - 3.9
"Minor"
Often felt, but rarely causes damage.

Noticeable shaking of indoor items. Significant damage unlikely.

Can cause major damage to poorly constructed buildings over small regions. At most slight damage to well-designed buildings.

Earthquakes produce multiple secondary effects that have an impact on people and their surroundings.
These include:
  • damage to structures like buildings, roads, bridges and water pipes
  • the creation of a new fault due to ground ruptures
  • tsunamis
  • liquefaction (decrease in the strength and stiffness of the soil) and landslides
  • electrical fires and gas leakage (Orallo, 2011)
The common safety measures that are taught are concerned with what to do during an earthquake and what to do after. But this does not mean that it is not possible to prepare for an earthquake before it even happens. Some of the steps both government and non-government organizations usually take are of a “curative nature” and are not enough by themselves.
Orallo (2011)

During an earthquake:

  • duck under a sturdy desk or table or protect your head with your arms
  • stay away from glass windows, shelves, cabinets and other heavy objects
  • be aware of falling objects
  • stay away from trees and power lines
  • move away from steep slopes that may be affected by landslides
  • if you’re near the shore, move quickly to higher ground in case a tsunami follows

After an earthquake:

  • be prepared for after shocks
  • don’t use elevators or enter damaged buildings
  • check yourself and others for injuries
  • check water and electrical lines for damages

But what about BEFORE the earthquake actually strikes?

It is said that prevention is better than cure but due to the difficulty in predicting earthquakes, people rarely or sometimes don’t even bother to know about precautionary earthquake measures.


Strap / bolt down heavy furniture to the walls.

Check the stability of hanging objects (e.g. Ceiling fans, chandeliers).

Familiarize yourselves with exit routes, locations of fire extinguishers / fire alarms and first aid kits.

Prepare an emergency supply kit with first aid, food, water, clothing, blankets, radios, flashlights and IDs.

Conduct and participate in regular earthquake drills.

Make sure your home / building is earthquake ready.
